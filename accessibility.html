Accessibility

Website accessibility is not a legal requirement in the United States, and the ADA has no set standard, but it can lead to lawsuits regardless. Understandably, this makes accessibility a key topic among web developers. So while the ADA may not have accessibility standards in place, the W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) has universally accepted protocols known as the WCAG.

Other countries have been more proactive about website accessibility. The European Union released new legislature in September of 2018, and that legislature has had a trickle-down effect on the fast-moving and globe-spanning frontier of the web. Accessibility is a hot topic right now due to this legal enforcement, but it’s not the only reason why developers are all about it.

Big businesses are recognizing that accessibility is key to a website’s design. Designing for inclusivity helps move a company’s reputation forward, and it feels like accessibility laws are an inevitable eventuality over the next few years. It is better to design for a change that you know is coming than scramble to patch it in afterwards.

What’s more, following the standards of the WCAG can just mean good design. The WCAG pushes for understandability, operability, and just overall clarity of a webpage. What is the purpose of a webpage after all, if not to convey information? Seeing information in clear formats helps everyone and makes for a better user experience.

This usability can help us convey important and sometimes complex information. Governments and businesses are relying more and more on the web to explain policy, answer questions, and automate tasks. If these sites lack accessibility, they prevent users who need to view a site for their own well-being from doing so. The world wide web and the websites we design are meant to be universal and public resources. It’s our job to make sure they’re truly universal.
